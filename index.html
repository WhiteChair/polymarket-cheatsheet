Longshot scalping — pros & cons
Pros
  • Huge % gains on small absolute moves — a 3¢ move can double your money
  • Limited downside per position (you only lose what you bet)
  • News catalysts move longshots more than favorites (asymmetric response)
  • Fees near the extremes are tiny — the fee formula collapses to near-zero below $0.10
Cons
  • Bid-ask spreads are wide — market might show $0.08 but you buy at $0.10 and sell at $0.06. Spread alone can kill the trade.
  • Thin liquidity — large orders move the price against you. Hard to exit at any size.
  • Most longshots resolve to zero. Win rate will be low even with edge.
  • Mental trap: seeing 20 bets at 5¢ feels cheap, but 20 × $50 = $1000 all going to zero is real
  • Requires repeated edge across many bets, not one "feels like a steal" pick
Rule of thumb
  • Use limit orders only (taker spreads destroy EV at these prices)
  • Size down — each longshot bet ≤1% of bankroll
  • Set exit targets before entering (e.g. "sell half at 3x, rest at 5x")

What not to do

Rule 1:Don't bet what you can't lose. Entertainment money only. If a loss ruins your week, your size is wrong.
Rule 2:Never ignore Rule 1.
Other traps to avoid
  • ✕Don't chase losses. Doubling down on the next market to "make it back" is the most expensive sentence in betting.
  • ✕Don't ignore resolution wording. "Will X happen by Y?" — does it mean announced, confirmed, or completed? Read carefully before clicking buy.
  • ✕Don't stack correlated bets. Three bets that all pay if same candidate wins = one big bet. You're not diversified, you're concentrated.
  • ✕Don't hold losers hoping. If your thesis breaks, exit. "Maybe it comes back" is how $100 becomes $5.
  • ✕Don't ignore volume and liquidity. This is the single most underrated factor.
    • Size relative to book: if you want to bet $500 and the ask side only has $300 at the shown price, you'll walk up the book and pay worse prices. That's slippage.
    • Exit matters more than entry: getting in is easy. Getting out when thesis breaks — or when you want to take profit — requires someone on the other side.
    • Low volume ≠ always bad: it can signal an inefficient market (the good kind, for patient bettors). But tread small — size in proportion to the depth you see.
    • Rule: your bet size < ~10% of shown book depth on both sides. Otherwise you're the market, not a participant in it.
  • ✕Don't treat thin markets like liquid ones. A $50k volume market may be a pricing error waiting to be corrected — or a trap with no exit. Small positions let you test without getting stuck.

Casual better — should I even care about fees?
Short answer: mostly no. If you're betting $5–$20 for fun, fees are genuinely small in absolute terms.
Bet $10 on politics at $0.50:
Fee (1% max rate):$0.10
If you win:payout $19.90
Fee as % of winnings:0.5%
The cost of fun: a $10 bet costs you roughly a dime in fees. That's less than a coffee tip. When fees DO matter for casuals:
  • Scalping in-and-out — 5 quick trades of $10 = $0.50 in fees. Now fees are 10%+ of a small gain.
  • Near-certain bets — paying $0.10 fee on a bet that only pays +11% is a bigger bite
  • Building positions over time — 20 small bets × $0.10 each = $2 gone to fees you never noticed
TL;DR: one-off $10 bet for fun = don't worry. Making 10 trades a week = start noticing. Using limit orders (maker) costs zero either way.
How Polymarket hides the fee from you
Polymarket doesn't show a fee line in the order confirmation. The fee is baked into the price — you see "Buy 100 shares at $0.60" and $60 leaves your wallet. But you actually receive slightly fewer shares than 100. The missing shares are the fee.
You wire:$60.00
Price per share:$0.60
Shares you'd expect (naive):100.00
Shares actually received (1%, taker):99.04
Hidden fee:$0.58
What to check: after every trade, compare shares received to stake ÷ price. Gap = your fee. Use limit orders to pay zero.

10-second checklist

  • 1
    Resolution wording clear? Read criteria. If vague, ambiguous, or "at discretion of..." → skip immediately. Oracle disputes eat winners.
  • 2
    Volume + liquidity real? <$100k volume or thin order book = rando opinion, not signal. Check depth at your size.
  • 3
    My estimate vs market? Say probability out loud before seeing price. Edge = gap ≥5%.
  • 4
    Resolution date? Long locks kill returns. Annualize before comparing to T-bills (~4%).
  • 5
    Exit liquidity? Can I sell mid-market if thesis breaks? Thin books = trapped.
